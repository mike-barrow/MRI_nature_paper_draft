\section*{Background \& Summary}\label{sec:background}

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is a non-invasive imaging technique that
provides high-resolution, soft-tissue-rich anatomical information without
exposing patients to ionising radiation. These properties make it central to the
diagnosis and monitoring of a wide range of conditions in clinical practice, and
particularly valuable in paediatric populations, where avoiding ionising
radiation is a primary concern [TBD: cite].

% Reusable framing: image-based AI -> upstream need for whole-body anatomical
% taxonomy -> adult-only / CT-dominated gap -> pediatric MRI need (mirrors the
% abstract argument). Expand here.
Image-based artificial intelligence (AI) tools are increasingly used across
clinical applications, and a key upstream capability for many of them is the
accurate identification of anatomy across the whole body. Progress on this task
has been enabled by several large imaging datasets; however, to date these have
been overwhelmingly limited to adult subjects and dominated by computed
tomography (CT) [TBD: cite]. This adult, CT-centric focus limits paediatric
adoption for two reasons. First, paediatric anatomy differs substantially from
adult anatomy across development, so models calibrated on adults alone behave
unpredictably on paediatric inputs [TBD: cite]. Second, because of children's
heightened sensitivity to ionising radiation, MRI is generally preferred over CT
in paediatric imaging, so adult CT corpora are of limited transfer value
[TBD: cite].

% Contribution paragraph.
To help address these entangled gaps in image coverage and appropriate modality,
we present a paediatric whole-body MRI dataset of [TBD: $\sim$300] examinations
spanning ages [TBD: 0--26] years. The dataset includes both radiologically clear
examinations and examinations exhibiting a range of pathologies, and is
distributed in NIfTI format together with [TBD: rich indexing metadata,
multi-reader anatomical labels, and (optionally) paired de-identified radiology
reports]. An overview of the cohort and the imaging protocols is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:cohort}.

% Reuse / impact paragraph: what the data can be used for.
We anticipate that this dataset will support the development and benchmarking of
paediatric whole-body anatomical analysis tools, including [TBD: anatomical
classification and segmentation, cross-modality transfer, and report-grounded
multimodal modelling]. By providing paediatric MRI with consistent organisation
and labelling, it complements existing adult, CT-dominated resources and lowers
the barrier to validating AI methods in paediatric settings.
