\section*{Background \& Summary}\label{sec:background}

Whole-body positron emission tomography combined with magnetic resonance imaging (PET/MRI) pairs soft-tissue-rich anatomical detail with a functional measure of metabolic activity.
The MRI component localizes and characterizes tissue without the ionizing radiation of computed tomography (CT), while the PET component quantifies tracer uptake and so separates active disease from treated or inactive tissue.
In pediatric oncology, and in lymphoma in particular, this pairing is well suited to diagnosis, staging, and treatment monitoring, where limiting cumulative radiation across repeated examinations is a primary concern~\cite{Kurch_2021,Masselli_2025}.

Image-based artificial intelligence (AI) increasingly supports these tasks, and a common prerequisite is the reliable identification of anatomy across the whole body, against which functional findings such as metabolically active lesions can be localized and scored.
Progress has been driven by several large public imaging datasets, but these remain overwhelmingly adult and dominated by anatomical CT, with little paired functional imaging~\cite{Wasserthal_2023,Gatidis_2022}.
This adult, CT-centric focus limits pediatric adoption for three reasons.
First, pediatric anatomy changes substantially across development, so models calibrated on adults behave unpredictably on pediatric inputs~\cite{Chatterjee_2025,Laborie_2026}.
Second, because children are more sensitive to ionizing radiation, MRI rather than CT is preferred for the anatomical component of pediatric whole-body imaging, so adult CT corpora transfer poorly~\cite{Brenner_2007,GalloBernal_2025}.
Third, anatomy alone does not capture disease activity, and the functional PET signal needed to detect lesions and assess treatment response is absent from these resources~\cite{Barrington_2014,Cheson_2014}.

To address these entangled gaps in coverage, modality, and functional labeling, we present a pediatric whole-body PET/MRI dataset of [TBD] examinations from [TBD] patients, spanning ages [TBD] years.
The collection includes both examinations with active lymphoma and disease-free follow-up examinations, and it is released in DICOM, with manually annotated segmentations stored as DICOM-SEG.
The released labels comprise multi-structure anatomical segmentations and tumor-lesion segmentations, together with indexing metadata that links each examination to its imaging and annotations.
An overview of the cohort and the imaging protocols is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:cohort}.

We anticipate that the dataset will support the development and benchmarking of pediatric whole-body imaging tools, including pediatric whole-body anatomical segmentation, PET-guided lesion detection and segmentation, and cross-modality and adult-to-pediatric transfer learning.
By providing pediatric PET/MRI with consistent organization and labeling, it complements existing adult, CT-dominated resources and lowers the barrier to validating AI methods in pediatric settings.
